Chapter 1: Weak Acids and \(K_a\) Calculations

Weak acids only partially dissociate in water. For a generic weak acid, \( \mathrm{HA} \), the equilibrium is:

$$\mathrm{HA} \rightleftharpoons H^+ + A^-$$

The acid dissociation constant is defined as:

$$K_a = \frac{[H^+][A^-]}{[HA]}$$

And the pKa is:

$$pK_a = -\log_{10}(K_a)$$

Chapter 2: Buffer Solutions

Buffers resist changes in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added. They consist of a weak acid and its conjugate base.

The Henderson–Hasselbalch equation is:

$$\mathrm{pH} = pK_a + \log_{10}\left(\frac{[A^-]}{[HA]}\right)$$

Chapter 3: Acid–Base Equilibrium Basics

Acid–base reactions involve proton transfers governed by equilibrium principles. Important concepts include:

  • Le Chatelier’s Principle: If a system at equilibrium is disturbed, it will shift to counter the change.
  • Autoionization of Water: $$\mathrm{H_2O \rightleftharpoons H^+ + OH^-} \quad \text{with} \quad K_w \approx 1.0 \times 10^{-14}$$
  • pH Relationship: $$\mathrm{pH + pOH = 14}$$ at 25°C.

These principles are fundamental for analyzing reaction conditions and designing buffer systems.

Chapter 5: Titrations & Additional Topics

Titration is a method used to determine the concentration of an unknown solution. Key points include:

  • Equivalence Point: When the titrant exactly neutralizes the analyte.
  • Half-Equivalence Point: Where half of the analyte has been neutralized – for a weak acid, pH = pKa.
  • Titration curves reveal the acid–base properties of the solution.

Mastery of titration concepts is essential for laboratory work and analytical chemistry.

Chapter 6: Trends in Acid Strength

Acid strength is influenced by the molecular structure. Two major factors include:

Electronegative Substituents

In oxyacids of the form \( \mathrm{HXO_n} \), as more oxygen atoms bond to the central atom, the electron density is pulled away from the acidic hydrogen. This stabilization of the conjugate base increases the acid’s strength.

Oxidation Number

For a series of oxyacids, a higher oxidation number of the central atom indicates a stronger electron-withdrawing effect, thus producing a stronger acid.

Example: Ranking Halogen Oxyacids

For 0.1 M solutions of:

  • \( \mathrm{HClO_2} \) (chlorous acid)
  • \( \mathrm{HBrO_2} \) (bromous acid)
  • \( \mathrm{HIO_2} \) (iodous acid)

The expected order of acidity is:

$$\mathrm{HClO_2} > \mathrm{HBrO_2} > \mathrm{HIO_2}$$

This order reflects the decreasing electronegativity from chlorine to iodine.

Chapter 7: Weak Bases and \(K_b\) Calculations

Like weak acids, weak bases only partially react with water. For a weak base \( \mathrm{B} \), the equilibrium is:

$$\mathrm{B + H_2O \rightleftharpoons BH^+ + OH^-}$$

The base dissociation constant is given by:

$$K_b = \frac{[BH^+][OH^-]}{[B]}$$

And the pKb is defined as:

$$pK_b = -\log_{10}(K_b)$$

Chapter 8: Factors Affecting Strong Acid Strength

Strong acids are defined by their nearly complete dissociation in water; however, subtle differences exist among them. Several factors determine why one strong acid can be stronger than another:

  • Bond Strength: Weaker bonds to hydrogen facilitate easier proton loss.
  • Electronegativity: A more electronegative central atom stabilizes the negative charge on the conjugate base.
  • Solvation Effects: The ability of water to stabilize the ions formed can vary with acid structure.

For example, while both HCl and HBr are strong acids, subtle differences in bond dissociation energies and solvation can lead to differences in their behavior in concentrated solutions.

Chapter 9: Titration Curves and Zones

Titration curves offer a graphical representation of the pH change during titration. Key regions include:

  • Initial Region: The starting pH of the analyte solution.
  • Buffer Region: A relatively flat area where pH changes slowly.
  • Equivalence Point: The point at which the amount of titrant equals the amount of analyte.
  • Post-Equivalence Region: pH changes rapidly after the equivalence point.
