Chapter 1: Weak Acids and Ka Calculations

Weak acids partially dissociate in water. The dissociation of a generic weak acid, \( \mathrm{HA} \), is given by:

$$\mathrm{HA} \rightleftharpoons H^+ + A^-$$

The acid dissociation constant is defined as:

$$K_a = \frac{[H^+][A^-]}{[HA]}$$

And the pKa is defined as:

$$pK_a = -\log_{10}(K_a)$$

Chapter 2: Buffer Solutions

Buffer solutions resist changes in pH upon the addition of small amounts of acid or base. A typical buffer is composed of a weak acid and its conjugate base.

The Henderson–Hasselbalch equation describes the pH of a buffer:

$$\mathrm{pH} = pK_a + \log_{10}\left(\frac{[A^-]}{[HA]}\right)$$

Chapter 3: Acid–Base Equilibrium Basics

Acid–base reactions involve proton transfer and are governed by equilibrium principles. Key topics include:

  • Le Chatelier's Principle: When a system at equilibrium is disturbed, it will shift to counteract the change.
  • Autoionization of Water: \( \mathrm{H_2O} \rightleftharpoons H^+ + OH^- \) with \( K_w = [H^+][OH^-] \approx 1.0 \times 10^{-14} \) at 25°C.
  • Relationship: \( \mathrm{pH} + \mathrm{pOH} = 14 \) (at 25°C).

Understanding these concepts is crucial for analyzing acid–base reactions, designing buffer systems, and calculating pH in different environments.

Chapter 4: pH Calculations

pH is a measure of the acidity of a solution and is defined as:

$$\mathrm{pH} = -\log_{10}([H^+])$$

You can also reverse this equation to calculate the hydrogen ion concentration from the pH.

Chapter 5: Titrations & Additional Topics

Titration is an analytical method used to determine the concentration of an unknown solution. In an acid–base titration:

  • The equivalence point is reached when the amount of titrant exactly neutralizes the analyte.
  • The half-equivalence point is when half of the analyte has been neutralized – here, \( \mathrm{pH} = pK_a \) for a weak acid.
  • Titration curves can reveal important information about the acid–base properties of the solution.

While a full titration simulation can be complex, understanding these principles is essential for laboratory analysis and practical chemistry.

Chapter 6: Trends in Acid Strength

Acid strength is influenced by several factors, including the electronegativity of atoms attached to the central atom and the oxidation state of the central atom.

Effect of Electronegative Substituents

When additional electronegative atoms bond to the central atom (Y) in an acid, they withdraw electron density via the inductive effect. This stabilizes the negative charge on the conjugate base and increases acid strength. For example, in a series of oxyacids of the form \( \mathrm{HXO_n} \), as the number of oxygen atoms increases, the conjugate base is better stabilized, making the acid stronger.

Effect of Oxidation Number

In a series of oxyacids, the acidity increases with the oxidation number of the central atom. A higher oxidation state indicates a stronger electron-withdrawing effect, further stabilizing the conjugate base.

Ranking of Halogen Oxyacids

Consider the 0.1 M solutions of the following oxyacids:

  • \( \mathrm{HClO_2} \) (chlorous acid)
  • \( \mathrm{HBrO_2} \) (bromous acid)
  • \( \mathrm{HIO_2} \) (iodous acid)

Based on the electronegativity of the halogen, the expected order of acid strength is:

$$\mathrm{HClO_2} > \mathrm{HBrO_2} > \mathrm{HIO_2}$$

Chlorine is more electronegative than bromine, and bromine is more electronegative than iodine. Therefore, the conjugate base of \( \mathrm{HClO_2} \) is most stabilized, making it the strongest acid among the three.
